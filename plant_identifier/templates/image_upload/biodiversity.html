Goldenrod (Solidago)

Goldenrods are a vital source of nectar for pollinators, especially in late summer and fall when many other plants have finished blooming. They attract bees, butterflies, and other insects. Goldenrods are host plants for several species of caterpillars, which are crucial for the diet of many bird species. The presence of goldenrods can increase the diversity and abundance of insect prey available for birds, especially during migration periods when energy demands are high.

Asters

The plants offer habitat and breeding grounds for a variety of insects. For example, certain species of butterflies may lay their eggs on or near aster plants, as the emerging caterpillars can feed on the leaves. Beyond serving as host plants for caterpillars, the seeds produced by aster plants serve as a food source for birds and small mammals, especially as other food sources become scarce in late fall and early winter.

Sunflowers (Helianthus spp.)

The seeds produced by sunflowers are an important food source for a wide range of wildlife, including birds (e.g., finches, chickadees, and doves) and small mammals (e.g., squirrels and mice). The presence of sunflowers can therefore support higher levels of biodiversity in terms of both plant and animal species.The structure of sunflower plants can offer habitat and shelter for various insects and small animals.

Violets (Viola)

The dense foliage of violets provides shelter and breeding sites for various insects and small ground-dwelling animals. For example, the caterpillars of some fritillary butterflies rely exclusively on violets as their host plants, making these plants critical for their survival. Beyond serving as host plants for specific butterfly species, violets can be a food source for various animals. Deer, rabbits, and other herbivores sometimes feed on the leaves and flowers.

Sedges (Carex)

While sedges are generally wind-pollinated, their presence in an ecosystem can support a variety of pollinators indirectly. By contributing to a diverse plant community, sedges help sustain populations of insects, including some pollinators that rely on the broader floral diversity within sedge-inhabited environments. Sedges are a food source for a range of wildlife. Their seeds are consumed by birds and small mammals, while the plants themselves can be foraged by larger herbivores. The dense sedge beds also provide hiding and nesting spots, crucial for the survival of various species.

Hawthorn (Crataegus)

The berries produced by hawthorns are an essential food source for many bird species, especially during late fall and winter when other food sources are scarce. Mammals, including small rodents and even larger animals like foxes, also consume the fruit, aiding in seed dispersal. Hawthorns can form dense thickets that provide shelter and nesting sites for birds and small mammals. These thickets can be crucial for the protection of some species from predators and harsh weather conditions.

Honeysuckle (Lonicera spp.)

The berries produced by honeysuckles serve as a food source for various bird species, as well as some small mammals. While they provide essential nutrients, especially in late summer and fall, it's worth noting that the berries of some honeysuckle species can be toxic to humans. Dense thickets formed by some shrubby honeysuckles provide shelter and nesting sites for birds and small mammals. The twining vines of climbing honeysuckles can also offer structural support for nesting.

Sedum

The wide range of species within the Sedum genus contributes to genetic diversity and adaptability. Sedums vary greatly in terms of size, leaf shape, and flower color, with species adapted to everything from dry, rocky soils to moist, forested areas. This diversity allows them to fill various ecological niches and support different community dynamics within ecosystems.

Buddlejas/Butterfly bushes

Beyond pollinators, the flowers and foliage of Buddleja species can provide habitat and food sources for a variety of other wildlife, including birds and small mammals in some regions. The dense growth of some Buddleja species can offer shelter and nesting sites. The wide range of species within the Buddleja genus contributes to genetic diversity, which is crucial for adaptability and resilience of plant populations to changing environments and threats such as diseases and pests.
